\section{Introduction}

This chapter presents the general context in which my end-of-study internship took place. It introduces Theodo Morocco as the host organization, its connection to the international Theodo Group, its work culture, and the values that guide its teams. It also presents the recruitment process, the integration experience, the project team, the overall mission of the internship, and an analysis of the existing compliance process that motivates this work.

The purpose of this chapter is not to describe the technical solution in detail, but to situate the internship in its professional environment. The internship subject belongs to the field of artificial intelligence applied to software quality, with a specific focus on automating the store-compliance auditing of mobile applications through an AI coding agent. Such an agent supports development teams by analyzing a codebase, reasoning over store policies, and reporting compliance issues early in the development cycle.

Working on such a subject requires more than technical experimentation. It requires a structured environment, clear communication, careful documentation, and continuous feedback. Theodo's culture provided a suitable framework for this type of mission, combining autonomy, rigor, collaboration, and a strong focus on practical impact.

\section{Host Organization}

Theodo Morocco is a technology consulting company specialized in building high-value digital solutions and supporting organizations in their transformation projects. The company was initially known as Nimbleways before joining the international Theodo Group in 2019. This integration connected the Moroccan team to a wider ecosystem composed of several specialized entities working in areas such as finance, healthcare, cloud, public services, data, and artificial intelligence.

Theodo Group follows a culture inspired by Lean Management. Its teams focus on continuous improvement, operational excellence, rapid feedback, and the delivery of measurable value. Work is organized around close collaboration with stakeholders, clear prioritization, and a constant effort to understand the real needs behind each project.

Within this ecosystem, Theodo Morocco plays an important role as a delivery and expertise hub. The company brings together profiles with strong analytical, product, and engineering skills, while maintaining a culture based on learning, ownership, and collective problem solving. This environment is particularly relevant for an internship related to artificial intelligence and agents, because such projects require both experimentation and disciplined execution.

\section{Values and Mission}

Theodo's values play a central role in the way teams work on a daily basis. They are not abstract principles; they are reflected in concrete practices such as task organization, feedback rituals, documentation, problem solving, and continuous improvement.

\subsection{Rigor}

Rigor means diligence and care in the work delivered. Clients expect the highest standards of professionalism, and this is reflected in concrete habits: daily to-do lists, regular reviews, systematic documentation of decisions, and continuous capitalization on learnings.

Mediocrity is not tolerated. This formality in processes helps ensure consistent and predictable results, sprint after sprint. In a compliance-auditing project driven by a language model, rigor is especially important because results must be reproducible, traceable to evidence, and documented in order to avoid approximate decisions or unsupported assumptions.

\subsection{Pragmatism}

Pragmatism consists of solving problems through structured methods rather than individual heroism. Discussions are grounded in concrete elements: data, observations, user feedback, and measurable results. Abstract debates have little value when a decision can be informed by an experiment, a prototype, or a real use case.

For a project based on an AI auditing agent, pragmatism helps keep the focus on usefulness. The objective is not simply to use an advanced technology, but to verify that the tool truly helps teams catch compliance issues earlier, reduce manual checking, and avoid costly store rejections.

\subsection{Eagerness to Progress}

Theodo considers problems as opportunities to learn. This eagerness to progress is notably expressed through the Andon system: any team member can signal a blocker in real time so that the group can address it c	ollectively, without delay and without trying to hide the issue.

This culture encourages transparency and accelerates learning. It is highly relevant for a store-compliance topic, where store guidelines, models, and best practices evolve quickly. Continuous learning becomes necessary to keep the policy rules up to date, understand model limitations, and improve results through iteration.

\subsection{Team Spirit}

Team spirit means that the collective interest comes before individual interests. Team members challenge each other to maintain high standards, and project success is attributed to the group as a whole. This creates an environment where trust and high expectations coexist.

In a mission involving an AI auditing agent, team spirit is essential. It helps validate assumptions, challenge the policy rules and the review workflow, improve the clarity of the reports, and avoid designing a solution from a single isolated perspective.

These values have been recognized by the independent organization ChooseMyCompany, which distinguished Theodo for the quality of its work environment and employee satisfaction.

\section{Recruitment Process}

Obtaining the internship opportunity at \textit{Theodo} involved a progressive recruitment process designed to evaluate technical reasoning, problem-solving ability, motivation, communication skills, and cultural fit.

The first step consisted of a technical assessment covering algorithmic thinking, logic, programming fundamentals, and problem solving. The objective was to evaluate the ability to reason clearly and structure a solution when facing different types of challenges.

The second step was an HR interview. This exchange allowed me to present my academic background, motivations, previous experiences, and interest in artificial intelligence topics. It was also an opportunity to better understand Theodo's culture, expectations, and working environment.

The process continued with more in-depth interviews focused on reasoning, communication, and the ability to explain a solution approach. These exchanges assessed not only knowledge, but also how I think, how I react to feedback, and how I approach unfamiliar problems.

The final discussions focused on the alignment between my profile, the company's values, and the expectations of the internship. This process confirmed my interest in joining an environment where quality, learning, collaboration, and user impact are central.

\section{Integration and Onboarding Process}

On my very first day at Theodo Morocco, I joined the \textbf{Asakai} meeting, the weekly ritual where each team presents its progress and the blockers it has encountered. Being placed in this ritual from the first morning immediately conveys the rhythm of the company.

The onboarding itself was organized around a starter project hosted on a \textbf{Kanban board in Notion}, Theodo's reference project-management tool. This project deliberately spans many areas of software development, from frontend to backend and testing. It is not meant to be easy: it is designed to confront newcomers with the company's standards as quickly as possible.

Several workshops complemented this immersion phase: an introduction to the \textbf{Andon} system, a presentation of the \textbf{Perfect User Story}, and a session on the \textbf{Daily Mail} as a tool for asynchronous communication.

What struck me most about this onboarding was its density. In a few weeks, you do not read how Theodo works, you live it.

\section{Internship Mission}

The mission entrusted to me during this internship is the design and development of the store-compliance component of the \textbf{Mobile Software Factory}, an internal tool that audits mobile applications against the publication rules of the Apple App Store and Google Play. The tool is run by a developer as a command during development: it inspects the source code and configuration of a cross-platform mobile application, applies the relevant store policies, and produces a clear report stating what was checked, what passes, what fails, and how each issue should be fixed.

Two design principles are central to the mission. The tool is \textbf{scope-aware}: it only evaluates the guidelines relevant to the features the application actually uses, so teams are not drowned in irrelevant rules. It is also \textbf{evidence-based}: every finding must be traced both to an observed code path and to a specific written policy rule, and when the evidence is insufficient, the finding is marked as unclear rather than guessed.

The most structuring objective of the project is to \textbf{shift compliance left}, that is, to detect issues early and continuously during development rather than at submission time. As a proof of concept, the scope was deliberately focused on the \textbf{permissions} domain, for both Android and iOS, on the Flutter, React Native, and Expo frameworks, with an architecture designed to welcome additional compliance domains later.

\section{Analysis of the Existing Compliance Process}

\subsection{A critical but late-handled step}

Store compliance is not a peripheral concern in mobile development. An application that violates a single store guideline can be rejected at submission, regardless of the quality of the rest of the work. Yet, in practice, this verification happens at the very end of the cycle: teams develop an application over weeks or months, and compliance is only checked when the application is uploaded to the stores. A rejection discovered at that point arrives at the worst possible moment, right before delivery.

\subsection{A manual, variable, and error-prone check}

The problem is not that compliance checks exist; it is the way they are performed. The Apple App Store Review Guidelines and the Google Play policies are long, detailed, and constantly evolving. Verifying them manually is repetitive and leaves room for omissions, especially when several applications are being developed in parallel. The result also depends heavily on the person performing the review: from one developer or one project to another, the depth and the consistency of the check vary significantly, and no automatic mechanism guarantees that every relevant rule has been considered.

\subsection{Impact on the delivery rhythm}

This late and manual approach has a direct impact on the delivery rhythm. A late rejection forces the team to revisit code and configuration that were considered finished, causing rework and delivery delays. Applications already in production accumulate a growing compliance debt as store requirements evolve, and risk removal if they fall out of conformity. Compliance therefore becomes a recurring, hidden bottleneck whose cause is not immediately visible.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter set the organizational and project context of my internship at Theodo Morocco. The company, with its Lean culture, its agile practices, and its demand for software quality, provides an environment in which a project such as the Mobile Software Factory fits naturally.

The four values of Theodo, namely rigor, pragmatism, eagerness to progress, and team spirit, form a solid foundation for a mission that requires both experimentation and disciplined, evidence-based execution.

The analysis of the existing compliance process confirms the reality of the problem: a manual, late, and variable verification that weighs on the delivery rhythm and exposes teams to costly store rejections. The following chapter presents the methodology adopted and the way the proposed solution fits into the development workflow.
